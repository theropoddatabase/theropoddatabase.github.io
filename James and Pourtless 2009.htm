Taxon issues

Proterosuchus- This includes P. fergusi, P. alexanderi and P. goweri, as all specimens were known and used in references the authors cite.
Hesperosuchus- Although Nesbitt (2011) could only asign the holotype to the taxon, the authors clearly used CM 29894 in addition, which was described as Hesperosuchus by Clark et al. (2000). As this specimen emerges in the same position as the holotype in Nesbitt's analysis, it is used here for coding as well.
"Syntarsus"- The authors say they used both rhodesiensis and kayentakatae, which is monophyletic here in the absence of other coelophysids.
Ceratosaurus- This includes C. magnicornis and C. dentisulcatus in addition to C. nasicornis, as indicated by the authors citing Madsen and Welles (2000).
Allosaurus- This includes the intended A. "jimmadseni" holotype, as indicated by the authors citing Chure (2001).
Sinraptor- This includes both S. dongi and S? hepingensis.
Nothronychus- This includes only N. mckinleyi.
Citipati- Both C. osmolskae and IGM 100/42 are used, as the latter is the source for codings in Clark et al. (2002).
Sinornithosaurus- This includes NGMC 91 as specified by the authors.
Unenlagia- Only U. comahuensis is used, based on the references cited by the authors.
Velociraptor- Only V. mongoliensis is used, based on references cited by the authors.
Saurornithoides- This includes both S. mongoliensis and Zanabazar, as specified by the authors.
Shuvuuia- This includes IGM 100/99 and 100/120, based on references cited by the authors.
Archaeopteryx- The authors note this includes all Solnhofen specimens.
Confuciusornis- This includes both C. sanctus and C. dui, based on references cited by the authors.
Sinornis- The authors state this includes Cathayornis yandica as well, so the holotypes of both genera are used.
Yanornis- Only Y. martini is used, based on references cited by the authors.
Enaliornis- This includes E. barretti, E? sedgwicki and E? seeleyi, based on references cited by the authors.

Coding issues

1. This character is a composite coding for elongate and tubular integumentary structures deriving from a follicle (the latter is true of all scale and feather homologs), those structures being wider distally, and having a calamus. As Voigt et al. (2009) showed the 'vanes' of Longisquama's parafeathers are actually lobes formed by membranes joined at their edges, and that the 'calamus walls' are actually the anterior and posterior lobes, only the elongate and basally tubular shape is shared between Longisquama and birds. Therefore, that is what I code for this character. Longisquama (Voigt et al., 2009), Dilong (Xu et al., 2004), Sinosauropteryx (Currie and Chen, 2001), Shuvuuia (Schweitzer et al., 1999), Rahonavis (Forster et al., 1998- based on quill knobs), Sinornithosaurus (Xu et al., 2001), Velociraptor (Turner et al., 2007- based on quill knobs), Sapeornis (Gao et al., 2012) and Yanornis (Zheng et al., 2013), have these. Juravenator (Chiappe and Gohlich, 2011) only preserves integument around its tail, which is at least partly scaled. Given Kulindadromeus, it's possible feathers and scales coexisted, though the evidence for preserved feathers in Juravenator is weak and may be collagen instead. The taxon is recoded unknown. Similarly, Compsognathus may have evidence of caudal scales (Peyer, 2006), and the lineations described by Ostrom (1978) may be internal structures or taphonomic. It is also recoded unknown.

2. This character codes for the symmetry of all "vaned integumentary structures", but in birds generally the remiges are asymmetrical while contour feathers are symmetrical. To prevent a largely meaningless polymorphic coding for most taxa, only remix symmetry is coded for here. The matrix incorrectly has states 1 and 2 instead of 0 and 1. Sapeornis (Gao et al., 2012) and Yanornis (Zheng et al., 2013) have asymmetrical retrices. Alligator and Longisquama (Sharov, 1970) should be coded inapplicable. The condition in Eoenantiornis has not been described if it is determinable in the specimen (Zhou et al., 2005).

3. Microraptor (Xu et al., 2003) and Protopteryx (Zhang and Zhou, 2000) have an alula. Its status in Archaeopteryx is controversial, as Bonde (2014) describes a possible alular feather in the London specimen. Alligator and Longisquama (Sharov, 1970) should be coded inapplicable.

4. This codes for "well developed, extensive" hindlimb feathers, in Archaeopteryx and Microraptor. As both have elongate tibial feathers, but only Microraptor has elongate metatarsal feathers, the former are coded for here. Yanornis (Zheng et al., 2013) has short tibiotarsal feathers. Sapeornis (Zheng et al., 2013) and Confuciusornis (Zheng et al., 2013) have long tibial feathers. Alligator should be coded inapplicable. Protopteryx (Zhang and Zhou, 2000) and Eoenantiornis (Zhou et al., 2005) are not preserved in a way that allows coding this character.
